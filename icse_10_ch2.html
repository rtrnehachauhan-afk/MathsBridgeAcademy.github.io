All Concepts & Formulae
📄1. Basic Terms▾
RECURRING DEPOSIT (R.D.) ACCOUNT
An account where a fixed sum of money (the instalment) is deposited every month for a fixed number of months. At the end of the term, the account "matures" — the depositor receives back everything deposited, plus interest.
SymbolMeaning
PMonthly instalment (the fixed sum deposited each month)
nNumber of months (the deposit is always monthly, so time must be converted to months)
rRate of interest, per annum (%)
M.V.Maturity Value — total amount received when the account matures
⚠️ MBA Note: n is always in MONTHS — convert years first (1 year = 12 months)
Converting time to months
"4 years" → n = 48. "2½ years" → n = 30. "3½ years" → n = 42. Always multiply the number of years by 12 before touching the formula.
🧮2. The Recurring Deposit Formula▾
Interest earned on the R.D. account
\( I = \dfrac{P\times n\times(n+1)\times r}{2\times12\times100} \)
This is simple interest on the sum of an arithmetic series of monthly balances — it is given directly, never derived from scratch in an exam answer.
Maturity Value
\( M.V. = P\times n \;+\; \dfrac{P\times n\times(n+1)\times r}{2\times12\times100} \)
Money paid in (P × n) plus the interest earned (I). Every single question in this chapter is a variation on this one line.
Which piece is usually missing
Most questions give you three of {P, n, r, M.V.} and ask for the fourth. Identify which one is unknown before you substitute — it decides whether you get a straight line (easy) or a quadratic (needs more care).
🔎3. Solving for the Unknown▾
UNKNOWN = r (rate)
P and n are known numbers, so M.V. = (known number) + (known number)×r, a straight line in r. Isolate r directly — no quadratic needed.
UNKNOWN = P (instalment)
n and r are known numbers, so M.V. = P×(known number) + P×(known number), i.e. M.V. = P×(a single combined number). Divide M.V. by that combined coefficient to get P directly.
⚠️ MBA Note: When solving for r or P, the equation is linear — resist the urge to reach for the quadratic formula
UNKNOWN = n (time)
Both n and n(n+1) appear, giving a quadratic in n. Expand fully, bring everything to one side, and either factorise or use the quadratic formula. Since n represents months, only the positive integer root is valid.
Sanity-check n
Once you solve for n, it should come out to a whole number of months (and usually a "nice" one — a multiple of 6 or 12). If it doesn't, re-check the expansion of n(n+1) before assuming the question is wrong.
🔁4. Common Question Patterns▾
COMPARING TWO ACCOUNTS
Work out each person's Maturity Value completely and separately using the same formula, then subtract at the very end to find who gets more and by how much. Never mix the two people's numbers mid-calculation.
"LEAST MONEY, IN A MULTIPLE OF ₹5"
Solve the equation for P normally — it usually won't come out as a round number. Since P must be a multiple of ₹5 and the target M.V. must be met or exceeded, round UP to the next multiple of ₹5, not to the nearest one.
⚠️ MBA Note: "Least ... to get at least ₹X" → round P UP to the next multiple, not to the nearest one
QUESTIONS FRAMED AROUND "INTEREST" (I) INSTEAD OF M.V.
Some questions state the interest earned rather than the maturity value directly. Use the Interest formula on its own first to find the missing quantity, then add P×n only at the end if the maturity value itself is also asked for.
Recap — Interest only
\( I = \dfrac{P\times n\times(n+1)\times r}{2\times12\times100} \)
"HOW MUCH MORE INTEREST IF DEPOSITED ₹X MORE PER MONTH"
Find the interest at the original P first, then recompute interest at the new (larger) P using the same n and r, then subtract. Never assume the extra interest scales in a simple proportion unless you check it against the formula.
