1. Distance Formula ▼

The distance between two points P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂) is:

PQ = √[(x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²]

This formula is derived using the Pythagoras theorem, treating the horizontal and vertical gaps between the points as the two legs of a right triangle.

2. Distance of a Point from the Origin ▼

The distance of a point P(x, y) from the origin O(0, 0) is a special case of the distance formula:

OP = √(x² + y²)
3. Distance of a Point from the Axes ▼

For a point P(x, y):

Distance from the x-axis = |y| (the ordinate)
Distance from the y-axis = |x| (the abscissa)
4. Section Formula (Internal Division, ratio m₁ : m₂) ▼

The coordinates of the point P(x, y) which divides the line segment joining A(x₁, y₁) and B(x₂, y₂) internally in the ratio m₁ : m₂ are:

x = m₁x₂+m₂x₁m₁+m₂   ,   y = m₁y₂+m₂y₁m₁+m₂

Here, PA : PB = m₁ : m₂.

5. Section Formula (ratio k : 1 form) ▼

If the point P divides AB in the ratio k : 1, the coordinates of P are:

x = kx₂+x₁k+1   ,   y = ky₂+y₁k+1

This form is especially convenient when the ratio itself is the unknown to be found — set up the equation, then solve for k directly.

6. Mid-point Formula ▼

The mid-point of the line segment joining P(x₁, y₁) and Q(x₂, y₂) is:

Mid-point = (x₁+x₂2 , y₁+y₂2)

This is the special case of the Section Formula where m₁ = m₂ = 1, i.e. the ratio is 1 : 1.

7. Key Applications ▼

Testing collinearity: Three points A, B, C are collinear if AB + BC = AC (the sum of the two shorter distances equals the longest).

Testing the type of triangle: Find all three sides using the Distance Formula. Equal sides mean isosceles; all different sides mean scalene; all equal sides mean equilateral. Apply the converse of the Pythagoras theorem (sum of squares of two sides = square of the third) to check for a right angle.

Testing the type of quadrilateral: Find all four sides and both diagonals.

Square: all sides equal, both diagonals equal
Rhombus: all sides equal, diagonals unequal
Rectangle: opposite sides equal, both diagonals equal
Parallelogram: opposite sides equal, diagonals bisect each other (use midpoint)
Kite: two pairs of adjacent sides equal

Area of a rhombus (using diagonals):

Area = 12 × d₁ × d₂

Finding the 4th vertex of a parallelogram: The diagonals of a parallelogram bisect each other, so the midpoint of one diagonal equals the midpoint of the other.

Finding a point on a given line/axis equidistant from two points, or the ratio in which an axis/line divides a segment: Use the Section Formula with the unknown ratio k : 1, then apply the condition (x = 0 for the y-axis, y = 0 for the x-axis, or substitute into the given line equation) to solve for k.
