📄1. Basic Terms & Definitions▾
RATIO
A comparison of two quantities of the same kind, written a : b or a/b. In a : b, "a" is the antecedent and "b" is the consequent.
PROPORTION
When two ratios are equal, the four quantities are said to be in proportion: a : b :: c : d (read "a is to b as c is to d"), i.e. a/b = c/d.
TermMeaning
ExtremesThe 1st and 4th terms — a and d
MeansThe 2nd and 3rd terms — b and c
Rule of Proportion
\( a:b::c:d \;\Rightarrow\; a\times d = b\times c \)
Product of extremes = Product of means. This is the single most useful fact in this chapter — every "find x" question in Q1–Q13 reduces to this cross-multiplication.
d is called
If a : b :: c : d, then d is the fourth proportional to a, b, c.
🔗2. Continued Proportion▾
CONTINUED PROPORTION
a, b, c are in continued proportion if a : b :: b : c, i.e. a/b = b/c. Here b is common to both ratios.
Mean Proportional
\( b^2 = a\times c \quad\Rightarrow\quad b=\sqrt{ac} \)
b is the mean (or geometric mean) proportional between a and c — always the positive square root of their product.
Third Proportional
\( a:b::b:c \)
Given a and b, c is the third proportional: c = b²/a.
The substitution trick
Whenever a, b, c are in continued proportion, the cleanest way to prove an identity is to substitute a = ck², b = ck (so a/b = b/c = k). This turns two unknowns into one (k), and every proof in this chapter's "prove that" section becomes a routine simplification.
⚠️ MBA Note: "In proportion" (4 terms: a,b,c,d with a/b=c/d) and "in continued proportion" (3 or more terms sharing a common middle term) are different setups — read the question carefully before choosing a substitution.
⚖️3. Properties of Proportion▾

These are the standard results used to solve "using properties of proportion" questions. Throughout, assume a/b = c/d.

Invertendo
\( \dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{b}{a}=\dfrac{d}{c} \)
Flip both sides.
Alternendo
\( \dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{a}{c}=\dfrac{b}{d} \)
Swap the means.
Componendo
\( \dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{a+b}{b}=\dfrac{c+d}{d} \)
Add 1 to both sides (add the denominator to the numerator on each side).
Dividendo
\( \dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{a-b}{b}=\dfrac{c-d}{d} \)
Subtract 1 from both sides.
Componendo–Dividendo (the workhorse)
\( \dfrac{a}{b}=\dfrac{c}{d} \;\Rightarrow\; \dfrac{a+b}{a-b}=\dfrac{c+d}{c-d} \)
By far the most-used property in this chapter. It is what turns a surd equation into a clean linear or polynomial one, and what proves "show a/b=c/d" identities in one line.
Why componendo-dividendo works so well on surds
If \( x=\dfrac{\sqrt P+\sqrt Q}{\sqrt P-\sqrt Q} \), rewrite it as \( \dfrac{x}{1}=\dfrac{\sqrt P+\sqrt Q}{\sqrt P-\sqrt Q} \). Applying componendo-dividendo gives \( \dfrac{x+1}{x-1}=\dfrac{2\sqrt P}{2\sqrt Q}=\dfrac{\sqrt P}{\sqrt Q} \) — the surds partially cancel, and one more squaring finishes the job.
🧭4. Problem-Solving Strategy▾
"FIND x" WITH A SINGLE FRACTION EQUATION
If the equation is just (one expression)/(another) = (a number), don't force componendo-dividendo — cross-multiply directly using the rule of proportion. This is faster and less error-prone.
SURD EQUATIONS OF THE FORM (√A+√B)/(√A−√B) = n
Apply componendo-dividendo once to get \( \dfrac{\sqrt A}{\sqrt B}=\dfrac{n+1}{n-1} \), square both sides to clear the surds, then solve the resulting linear or quadratic equation in x.
"PROVE a/b = c/d GIVEN ..." IDENTITIES
These almost always have the disguised form (pa+qb)/(pc+qd) = (pa−qb)/(pc−qd). Cross-multiplying this general form always collapses to ad = bc — i.e. a/b = c/d — regardless of the constants p, q. Recognising this shape saves a lot of algebra.
⚠️ MBA Note: When a "prove" question gives you an equation of the shape (X+Y)/(X−Y) = (Z+W)/(Z−W), that is componendo-dividendo already applied — your job is to run it in reverse (cross-multiply) to strip it back to the plain proportion.
CONTINUED-PROPORTION PROOFS (a,b,c or a,b,c,d)
Substitute a = ck², b = ck for three terms in continued proportion (or the equivalent chain of powers of k for four terms), then simplify both sides independently and show they match.
x DEFINED BY A SURD EXPRESSION, THEN "SHOW A QUADRATIC/CUBIC HOLDS"
Write \( x=\dfrac{\sqrt P+\sqrt Q}{\sqrt P-\sqrt Q} \) as \( \dfrac{x}{1} \), apply componendo-dividendo to isolate \( \dfrac{\sqrt P}{\sqrt Q} \) or a simplified ratio, square (or cube, for cube-root versions), and substitute back into the target equation to confirm it holds.
