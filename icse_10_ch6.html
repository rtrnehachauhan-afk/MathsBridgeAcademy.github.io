📄1. Basic Terms▾

A polynomial in x is an expression made up of terms with non-negative whole-number powers of x, such as \( f(x) = 2x^3+3x^2-x-2 \). The highest power of x present is called the degree of the polynomial.

SymbolMeaning
f(x)The polynomial written as a function of x
aThe value of x that makes the divisor (x − a) equal to zero
f(a)The value obtained by substituting x = a into f(x)

Every division has a divisor, a quotient, and a remainder — exactly like dividing whole numbers. For polynomials, this is written using the Division Algorithm:

DIVISION ALGORITHM
\( f(x) = (x-a)\times Q(x) + R \), where Q(x) is the quotient and R is the remainder.
➗2. Remainder Theorem▾
REMAINDER THEOREM
If a polynomial f(x) is divided by (x − a), the remainder is exactly f(a) — no long division needed.

Why it works: from the Division Algorithm, \( f(x) = (x-a)\times Q(x) + R \). Substituting \( x = a \) makes the first term vanish, since \( (a-a) = 0 \), leaving \( f(a) = R \).

Quick Example
Find the remainder when \( f(x) = x^2+3x+1 \) is divided by \( (x-1) \).
Divisor \( (x-1)=0 \Rightarrow x=1 \). \( f(1) = 1+3+1 = 5 \). ∴ Remainder = 5.

For a divisor like \( (2x-1) \), set \( 2x-1=0 \Rightarrow x = \tfrac12 \) and substitute that fraction — this is exactly the pattern used in Q1(iii).

✔3. Factor Theorem▾
FACTOR THEOREM
\( (x-a) \) is a factor of f(x) \( \Leftrightarrow \) \( f(a) = 0 \). It is just the Remainder Theorem for the special case where the remainder is zero.
💡 Number analogy (from the lecture notes)
  • Take \( p(x) = x^2-5x+6 \).
  • Compare it to normal division of numbers.
  • 10 ÷ 5 leaves remainder 0.
  • So 5 is a factor of 10.
  • 10 ÷ 3 leaves remainder 1.
  • So 3 is not a factor of 10.
  • Polynomials work the same way.
  • Put x = 2 into p(x).
  • \( p(2) = 4-10+6 = 0 \).
  • Remainder is 0.
  • So (x − 2) is a factor of p(x).
  • Now put x = 4 into p(x).
  • \( p(4) = 16-20+6 = 2 \).
  • Remainder is 2, not 0.
  • So (x − 4) is not a factor of p(x).
  • Rule: remainder 0 means factor. Remainder not 0 means not a factor.

This single idea — "substitute and check for zero" — is what Q6, Q7, Q8, Q9, and every board MCQ on this topic (B1, B3, B5) is built on.

🔍4. Finding an Unknown Coefficient▾

Whenever a question gives a remainder (or says "factor") and asks for an unknown letter like a, b, or k, the same four-step recipe applies:

  1. Set the divisor equal to zero and solve for x.
  2. Substitute that value of x into f(x) in place of x.
  3. Set the result equal to the given remainder (or to 0, if it's a factor condition).
  4. Solve the resulting equation for the unknown.

When two unknowns appear (e.g. a and b), you need two conditions — each one gives its own equation, and the two equations are solved simultaneously (by elimination or substitution). This is the pattern behind Q4, Q8, Q10, Q13, and board question B7.

🧩5. Factorising a Cubic Completely▾

To factorise a cubic like \( 2x^3-9x^2-11x+30 \) completely:

  1. Find one root by trial. Try values that divide the constant term (e.g. ±1, ±2, ±3, ±5...) until \( f(a) = 0 \).
  2. Confirm it's a factor using the Factor Theorem — this also proves the step, which board questions often ask for explicitly.
  3. Divide the cubic by that linear factor (long division or synthetic division) to get a quadratic quotient.
  4. Factorise the quadratic by splitting the middle term, giving the final three linear factors.
Where this is used
Q11, Q12, Q13, Q14, Q15 in the Exercise tab, and B4, B8, B9 in Board PYQs all follow exactly this four-step process.
Formulae used throughout
Remainder Theorem: \( f(x) \div (x-a) \Rightarrow \text{Remainder} = f(a) \)
Factor Theorem: \( (x-a) \) is a factor of \( f(x) \Leftrightarrow f(a) = 0 \)
