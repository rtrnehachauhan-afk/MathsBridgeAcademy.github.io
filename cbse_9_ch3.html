ℕ1. Natural Numbers, Whole Numbers & Integers▼
NATURAL NUMBERS (N)
The counting numbers {1, 2, 3, …}. No last natural number exists.
BRAHMAGUPTA'S RULES (628 CE)
Positive numbers = fortunes (dhana). Negative numbers = debts (ṛiṇa).
fortune + fortune = fortune  ·  debt + debt = debt
debt × fortune = debt  ·  debt × debt = fortune
ℚ2. Rational Numbers▼
DEFINITION
Any number of the form pq, where p, q are integers and q ≠ 0.
Density Property
Between any two rational numbers, infinitely many rational numbers exist.
Method used in this chapter — LCM/scaling (no averaging)
To find rationals between a/b and c/d: scale both to a common denominator large enough that enough integers exist strictly between the two scaled numerators, then read those integers off as the new numerators.
√3. Irrational & Real Numbers▼
PROOF BY CONTRADICTION — TEMPLATE (for √p, p prime)
Step 1 — Assume the Contrary: Suppose √p = ab, a,b co-prime, b≠0
Step 2 — Square: p·b² = a² ⟹ p divides a² ⟹ p divides a, so a = pc
Step 3 — Substitute: b² = p·c² ⟹ p divides b
Step 4 — Contradiction: p divides both a and b, contradicting a,b co-prime ∎
Key Properties
Rational ± Irrational = Irrational
Non-zero Rational × Irrational = Irrational
Irrational ± Irrational = need not be irrational (e.g. √3 + (−√3) = 0)
⟳4. Decimal Expansions▼
Terminating vs Repeating Test
Write pq in lowest form. If the only prime factors of q are 2 and/or 5, the decimal terminates. If q has any other prime factor, the decimal repeats.
CYCLIC NUMBERS
17 = 0.142857 — multiplying 142857 by 1–6 gives the same six digits cyclically shifted.
📜5. The History Behind This Chapter▼
HIPPASUS — c. 400 BCE
A member of the Pythagorean school, Hippasus gave the first known proof that √2 is irrational — using the same proof-by-contradiction method taught in this chapter. Legend says this discovery deeply unsettled the Pythagoreans, who believed all numbers were ratios of integers.
ĀRYABHAṬA — 499 CE
Gave the highly accurate approximation π ≈ 39271250 = 3.1416, but explicitly called it an āsanna ("approximation") — he suspected no exact fraction for π could ever be found, centuries before this was proven.
JOHANN LAMBERT — 1761
The Swiss mathematician who finally proved π is irrational, confirming Āryabhaṭa's centuries-old suspicion — no fraction, however large, can ever equal π exactly.
MĀDHAVA OF SANGAMAGRĀMA — 14th century
Founder of the Kerala School of Mathematics. Realised that an irrational number like π cannot be captured by any single fraction — only by an infinite sum. His series:
π = 4 × (1 − 1/3 + 1/5 − 1/7 + …)
was rediscovered in Europe roughly 300 years later and is now usually called the Leibniz series.
Why this matters
Every rule in this chapter — Brahmagupta's sign laws, the proof that √2 is irrational, the decimal tests — was worked out by real mathematicians centuries before calculators existed, often using nothing but logic and geometry.

📐Quick Reference — all rules used in this chapter▼
Brahmagupta's Sign Rules
fortune+fortune=fortune · debt+debt=debt · debt×fortune=debt · debt×debt=fortune
HCF Simplification
Divide numerator and denominator by their HCF to reach lowest form.
Distributive Law
p × (q + r) = p×q + p×r
Terminating Decimal Test
Denominator (lowest form) has only 2 and/or 5 as prime factors ⟹ terminates.
Finding Rationals Between Two Numbers
LCM/scaling method — never averaging. For whole-number gaps, insert extra decimal places directly (e.g. between 3 and 4: 3.1, 3.01, 3.001).
