📄1. Basic Terms▾
FACE VALUE (F.V.) / NOMINAL VALUE
The printed value of one share, fixed by the company when the share is issued (commonly ₹10, ₹25, ₹50, ₹100). Dividend is always calculated on the Face Value, never on the Market Value.
⚠️ MBA Note: Dividend is on F.V., never on M.V.
MARKET VALUE (M.V.)
The price at which a share is actually bought or sold in the market. This can be equal to, above, or below the Face Value.
ConditionRelation
At ParM.V. = F.V.
Above Par (Premium)M.V. = F.V. + Premium
Below Par (Discount)M.V. = F.V. − Discount
CHECKLIST — WHEN A DISCOUNT % IS GIVEN
Two steps, always in this order: (a) find the Discount in ₹ first — Discount = Rate of Discount × F.V. ÷ 100. (b) then find M.V. = F.V. − Discount. Don't skip straight to guessing M.V.
⚠️ MBA Note: Discount % → find ₹ discount first, then M.V.
Reading a share quote
"₹100 shares at ₹120" means F.V. = ₹100, M.V. = ₹120 — bought at a premium of ₹20. "₹50 shares at a discount of ₹6" means F.V. = ₹50, M.V. = ₹44.
DECODING THE WORDING — WHICH VALUE IS WHICH
Board questions often hide F.V. and M.V. inside ordinary sentences instead of labelling them. Use these keyword rules to tell them apart at a glance:
Keyword before a valueIt means
"on", "in", "of" (e.g. "dividend on ₹100 shares")That value is the F.V.
"at" (e.g. shares "at ₹120")That value is the M.V.
"paid" (e.g. "the price paid")Means M.V.
"rate/%" immediately followed by "investment"That is the % Return, not the dividend rate
📄1a. Profit on Sale of Shares▾
⚠️ MBA Note: "at" = M.V., "on/in/of" = F.V.
Profit (when shares are sold)
\( \text{Profit} = \text{Sale Proceeds} - \text{Investment} \)
⚠️ MBA Note: Profit ≠ Dividend — don't mix the two
Not the same as Dividend
Dividend is the yearly income a share earns while you hold it. Profit is the one-time gain (or loss) made only if and when you later sell the share for more (or less) than you paid for it. A question can ask for both in the same problem — keep them separate.
🧮2. Key Formulae▾
Investment
\( \text{Investment} = \text{No. of shares}\times \text{M.V. of 1 share} \)
Number of shares
\( \text{No. of shares} = \dfrac{\text{Total Amount Invested}}{\text{M.V. of 1 share}} \)
Number of shares — alternate route (when Investment isn't given directly)
\( \text{No. of shares} = \dfrac{\text{Total Dividend Received}}{\text{Dividend on 1 share}} \)
Useful whenever the question gives you the total dividend earned instead of the amount invested.
Dividend (Annual Income)
\( \text{Dividend} = \text{No. of shares}\times \dfrac{\text{Rate of Dividend}}{100}\times \text{F.V.} \)
Rate of Return (Income %)
\( \text{Rate of Return} = \dfrac{\text{Dividend}}{\text{Investment}}\times 100 \)
The Shortcut Formula (very frequently used)
\( \text{Rate of Dividend}\times \text{F.V.} = \text{Rate of Return}\times \text{M.V.} \)
Both sides equal the dividend earned by ONE share, just written two different ways — so they must be equal. This shortcut lets you jump straight from one unknown (rate, F.V., return, or M.V.) to another without going through the full investment.
Which formula to reach for
If the question gives Total Investment, start with "No. of shares". If it gives only rates/percentages and one price, the shortcut formula is faster. If it gives the Dividend amount directly, divide by (Rate × F.V./100) to get No. of shares first.
🔁3. Selling & Reinvesting (Portfolio-change Chains)▾
THE PATTERN
A person already owns shares in Company A. They sell all of them at a given Selling Price (S.P.), and the entire sale proceeds become the new Total Investment for Company B. The two companies are solved one after another using the same formulae — never mix the two companies' numbers together.
Sale Proceeds
\( \text{Sale Proceeds} = \text{No. of shares held}\times \text{S.P. of 1 share} \)
This Sale Proceeds becomes the new "Total Amount Invested" for the second company.
Change in Income
\( \text{Change in Income} = \text{Greater Dividend} - \text{Smaller Dividend} \)
Work out both dividends first, then subtract the smaller from the larger — don't assume the new dividend is always bigger. If the new dividend turns out smaller, the "change" is a decrease, and the question is usually asking by how much income fell.
⚠️ MBA Note: Change in Income = Greater − Smaller (not always New − Old)
Keep the two companies visually separate
Every chain question below is worked as "Company A" fully, then "Company B" fully, then combined only in the final step (change in income, or % change on the original investment).
CHECKLIST — "INVESTED THE PROCEEDS"
Whenever a question says a person sold shares and invested the proceeds elsewhere, you always need two numbers before anything else: (a) the Sale Proceeds, and (b) the New Investment in Company B, where New Investment = Sale Proceeds.
⚠️ MBA Note: "Invested proceeds" → New Investment = Sale Proceeds (unless it says "including dividend")
SPECIAL CASE — "INVESTED THE PROCEEDS INCLUDING DIVIDEND"
If the wording adds "including dividend", the year's dividend from Company A is added to the sale proceeds before reinvesting:
New Investment (proceeds + dividend case)
\( \text{New Investment} = \text{Sale Proceeds} + \text{Dividend} \)
